Which Era of Home Appreciates Fastest?

Median annualized appreciation from repeat sales, grouped by construction decade

1950s-era homes lead at 8.3% annual appreciation, likely because they're small, affordable, and in walkable older neighborhoods that have seen strong demand. 2000s homes trail at 5.4%.

How Often Do Longmont Homes Change Hands?

How many times each unique address has sold since 2006

61.6% of Longmont homes have only sold once since 2006, meaning most current owners have held on through the entire boom-and-correction cycle. Only 2.5% have sold four or more times.

Competition by Home Style

Percent sold above list price, 2020 to 2026

Tri-levels draw the most multiple-offer activity at 49.7%, likely because they're the most affordable style in many neighborhoods. Two-stories are the least competitive at 35.6%.

Appreciation by Home Style

Median annualized repeat-sale appreciation by architectural style

Bi-level/split homes have appreciated fastest at 7.8% annually. Two-stories trail at 6.0%, partly because they tend to be newer, higher-priced homes where percentage gains compress.

Where's the Most Competition by Price?

Percent sold above list price by listing-price range, 2020 to 2026

Homes listed under $400K see bidding wars 53.3% of the time. Above $1M, that drops to under 20%. The affordable end of the market is still the most competitive.

The Square-Footage Sweet Spot

Percent sold above list by total square footage, 2020 to 2026

The 1,500-1,799 sqft range is the most competitive at 45.4% sold above list. Homes over 3,200 sqft see the least competition at 30.9%.

Best Closing Month for Bidding Competition

Historical percent of sales that closed above list, by closing month (2006-2026)

May closings have the highest above-list rate at 34.5%, with April and June right behind. Since contracts close 30-45 days after going under contract, listing in March or April puts you in that sweet spot.
